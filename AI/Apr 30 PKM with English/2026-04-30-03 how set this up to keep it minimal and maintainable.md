My advice for managing this book focuses on **utility**. Since your ultimate goal is a scientific workshop, we should treat the textbook as a fuel source for your professional skills, not just a school assignment.

Here is how I recommend you set this up to keep it **minimal** and **maintainable**:

### 1. The "Single Source" Strategy

Don't create a folder for the book. Create **one** comprehensive Source Note:

`03_RESOURCES/Sources/New_Exploring_Reading_Writing_1.md`

Use this note as your "Command Center." Instead of making 20 small notes for 20 chapters, keep your reading notes and progress in this one file. This reduces "folder fatigue."

### 2. The "Vocabulary Graduation" Workflow

Don't let vocabulary die inside the book notes.

- **Step 1:** List new words in the book note while reading.
    
- **Step 2:** Every Sunday, move the words you _actually_ want to use in your workshop to a "Golden List" in `02_AREAS/English/Scientific_Lexicon.md`.
    
- **Why?** This separates "words I need to know for the test" from "words I need to lead a workshop."
    

### 3. Handle Essays as "Outputs"

When the book asks you to write an essay:

- Create the note in `05_OUTPUTS/Drafts/[Essay_Title].md`.
    
- **The Link:** At the top of the essay, link back to the book: `Source: [[New_Exploring_Reading_Writing_1]]`.
    
- **The Benefit:** This keeps your "work" (the essay) separate from your "study" (the book), making it easier to find and reuse your writing later when drafting your actual scientific paper.
    

### 4. Optimize for the "Scientist Workshop"

Since your constraint is English-first and your goal is a workshop:

- Add a section in your Source Note called **"Presentation Patterns."** * Every time the book uses a phrase like _"In conclusion"_ or _"The data suggests,"_ write it down there. These are the tools you will use when speaking to an audience.
